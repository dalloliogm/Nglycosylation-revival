# Manuscript Draft

Working title: Robustness and evolvability across a glycosylation pathway: N-glycosylation as a case study in evolutionary architecture

Date started: 2026-06-06

Draft status: first-pass Introduction, Methods, and Results. Claims and wording should be checked against `docs/manuscript/evidence-matrix.md` before expanding Discussion.

## Introduction

Biological systems must solve a recurrent architectural problem. They must preserve functions whose failure would compromise viability, while retaining enough flexibility to generate phenotypic variation in changing environments. Robustness and evolvability are therefore often discussed as system-level properties rather than attributes of single genes. Robustness can arise from buffering, modularity, feedback, degeneracy, and network organization, while evolvability depends on the capacity to generate heritable phenotypic variation that can be exposed, filtered, or reused under new conditions [kitano_biological_robustness; felix_wagner_pervasive_robustness; edelman_gally_degeneracy; payne_wagner_causes_evolvability]. A central unresolved question is whether these properties are spatially organized within biological pathways. If a pathway contains both catastrophic core functions and environmentally exposed output functions, then robustness and evolvability may be localized to different regions of the same system.

N-glycosylation provides a tractable case study for this question because it has a strongly ordered biochemical architecture and a biologically diverse output layer. Early steps construct a lipid-linked oligosaccharide precursor, transfer it to nascent proteins through the oligosaccharyltransferase machinery, and connect glycan processing to endoplasmic reticulum folding and quality-control systems. These steps affect broad glycoprotein maturation and therefore have plausible catastrophic-failure potential [stanley_nglycan_biosynthesis; helenius_aebi_nglycan_er; aebi_nglycosylation_er]. Later Golgi processing, branching, and terminal modification generate diverse mature glycan structures whose consequences are often deployed at protein surfaces, cell surfaces, secreted proteins, and tissue-specific contexts. This downstream layer is biologically connected to immune regulation, host-pathogen interaction, inflammation, cancer biology, mucosal interfaces, and glycan-mediated cell communication [varki_golgi_evolution; marth_grewal_immunity; rabinovich_glycan_immunity; poole_glycointeractions_bacterial_pathogenesis; reily_nglycosylation_inflammation; smith_cummings_virus_host_glycans].

This architecture motivates a testable model: N-glycosylation may preserve robustness in core and checkpoint-linked machinery while exposing evolvability through downstream glycan-output, regulatory, tissue-specific, and organism-environment interface layers. The model does not require downstream genes to be weakly constrained in protein-coding sequence, nor does it require every downstream association to represent local adaptation. Prior work already argues against such a simple view. Molecular-evolution and network studies of N-glycosylation found strong conservation and network-associated purifying constraint, while glyco-regulation work emphasized that glycan phenotypes arise from regulated networks of glyco-genes rather than from one-to-one template encoding [montanucci_primate_nglycosylation_network; zoldos_epigenetic_glycosylation; lauc_zoldos_genes_environment; kim_centralized_modularity]. In this framing, coding conservation and evolvability are not opposites. Evolvability may be expressed through glycan-output variation, regulatory control, expression context, and downstream trait effects even when many enzymes remain under coding constraint.

The historical starting point for this project is the 2012 analysis of positive selection and population differentiation in the asparagine N-glycosylation pathway [dallolio_nglycosylation_selection]. That study raised the possibility that evolutionary signals are unevenly distributed across pathway regions, but its population-genetic evidence is not a sufficient foundation for the modern argument. Older SNP-array resources, continental groupings, one-versus-rest differentiation summaries, fixed windows around genes, linkage disequilibrium, and demographic confounding all limit causal interpretation. The stronger modern contribution is therefore not to reproduce every original statistic. Instead, the original analysis is treated as historical context and a hypothesis generator for a broader architecture test.

Here, we formalize N-glycosylation as a pathway-level case study of robustness and evolvability. We curate a modern gene and pathway-edge representation, classify genes into biologically motivated pathway regions, and evaluate whether architecture predicts multiple evidence layers: gene-level constraint, severe Mendelian and congenital-disorder burden, ClinVar pathogenic variation, glycome and complex-trait associations, and downstream interface-related signals. The primary claim is deliberately cautious. We ask whether N-glycosylation supports a layered architecture in which severe disease evidence concentrates in shared core machinery while downstream branching and terminal-modification regions are richer in glycan-output and interface trait evidence. We do not claim that pathway position proves adaptation, that GWAS locus annotations are causal, or that N-glycosylation establishes a universal biological design principle.

The architecture model makes four predictions that can be checked against independent data and that distinguish it from a null model in which pathway position has no systematic effect. First, disruption of upstream-core genes should cause qualitatively more severe human disease than disruption of downstream-diversification genes; a null result — uniform CDG burden across regions — would argue against a catastrophic-core architecture. Second, downstream genes should be disproportionately associated with quantitative glycan-output phenotypes and organismal-interface traits relative to upstream genes, after controlling for gene count and expression breadth; a null result here would argue that any disease-burden gradient is simply an essentiality artifact rather than an interface-layer signal. Third, coding-sequence constraint, conservation, and population-differentiation statistics should not show a simple upstream-more-constrained gradient, because the robustness/evolvability model specifically predicts that evolvability operates through regulatory and output channels rather than coding tolerance; a strong coding gradient would suggest simpler explanations suffice. Fourth, if N-glycosylation's layered architecture is a property of its specific topology — conserved shared core feeding a combinatorial interface-output layer — then pathways lacking that topology (strictly linear pathways, single-product pathways) should not show the same disease-burden and trait-profile separation; a universal pattern across all pathway types would reduce the finding to a trivial consequence of shared-step essentiality. This study tests all four predictions and treats confirmation of each as evidence for the architecture model and falsification of any as evidence requiring model revision.

## Methods

This study was designed as a pathway-level computational case study rather than a close reproduction of the original population-genetic analysis. The analysis unit was a human gene assigned to a curated N-glycosylation pathway role. All curated inputs, generated tables, figures, and interpretive notes were stored in the project repository, with scripts used for repeatable transformations. The live project tracker, methods notes, and claim-control documents were maintained alongside the analysis so that changes in scope, provenance, and interpretation remained visible during drafting.

We first defined a modern N-glycosylation gene set from Reactome release 96 and the N-glycan biosynthesis chapter of Essentials of Glycobiology. Reactome event `R-HSA-446203`, Asparagine N-linked glycosylation, was used as the top-level source, with child events covering precursor biosynthesis and transfer, oligosaccharyltransferase activity, ER trimming and calnexin/calreticulin cycling, Golgi processing, antenna elongation, complex N-glycan synthesis, and hybrid N-glycan synthesis. The goal was not to include every Reactome participant attached to the broad parent pathway. Generic ER-Golgi transport genes, cargo proteins, and broad trafficking components were excluded unless they directly supported the architecture question. Genes were retained in the main analysis when they directly participated in lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, transfer of the N-glycan to nascent protein, ER glycan trimming or reglucosylation, Golgi mannose trimming, GlcNAc branching, core fucosylation, galactosylation, sialylation, or other curated terminal N-glycan modification.

Each gene was assigned a fine pathway-region label and a coarser analysis group. Fine labels distinguished substrate biosynthesis, lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, ER quality control, Golgi core processing, Golgi branching, and terminal modification. Coarse analysis groups were defined as upstream core, checkpoint layer, downstream diversification, and substrate support. The default primary contrast was upstream core versus downstream diversification. Checkpoint-layer and substrate-support genes were kept separate unless a sensitivity analysis explicitly merged them. Ensembl identifiers and coordinate fields in the first gene table were populated from MyGene.info and treated as provisional until direct Ensembl and HGNC cross-checking is completed.

To represent pathway architecture, we built a gene-level edge table rather than an atom-level biochemical reaction graph. Nodes were genes. Edges represented shared lipid-linked oligosaccharide intermediates, glycoprotein processing steps, donor-substrate support, quality-control cycling, or complex and binding-context relationships. Reactome was the primary edge source, with KEGG `hsa00510` used as supporting pathway-level evidence for donor-substrate and enzyme-class context. Because downstream Golgi N-glycan processing is combinatorial and partly represented by generic reactions in pathway databases, several downstream edges intentionally used generic labels such as branched N-glycan acceptor or terminal N-glycan acceptor. Donor-substrate edges were flagged because they can inflate graph density and should be handled separately in topology sensitivity analyses.

Architecture features were then computed or curated from the gene and edge tables using `scripts/build_nglyco_architecture_features.py`. The resulting feature table included pathway depth, scaled depth, upstream-core, checkpoint, downstream-diversification, and substrate-support indicators, checkpoint proximity, membership in lipid-linked oligosaccharide or oligosaccharyltransferase core machinery, ER quality-control membership, branching role, terminal-modification role, glycan-output-layer status, paralog-family labels, redundancy priors, low-specificity terminal-enzyme flags, and graph metrics. Graph-derived centrality metrics were treated as exploratory because they depend on graph encoding. Interpretable architecture descriptors such as pathway region, checkpoint proximity, branch role, terminal modification, and substrate-support status were given priority in the manuscript.

Gene-level coding constraint was analyzed with a reproducible importer and summarizer in `scripts/build_nglyco_constraint_summary.py` and a group-comparison script in `scripts/analyze_constraint_gradient.py`. The intended primary source was gnomAD v4.1.1 gene constraint metrics; during the first implementation pass, the available local file was `gnomad.v4.1.constraint_metrics.tsv`, downloaded from the gnomAD public release path and labeled as gnomAD v4.1. The constraint results are therefore provisional until the exact v4.1.1 export is identified or the v4.1 choice is formally accepted. Constraint metrics were joined to pathway genes by Ensembl gene ID with symbol cross-checks, and missing matches were retained in a join-audit table rather than silently dropped. The primary metrics were LOEUF and missense Z, with observed/expected loss-of-function and missense metrics retained where available.

Constraint comparisons were descriptive and effect-size focused. The primary contrast compared upstream-core genes with downstream-diversification genes. Sensitivity contrasts merged the checkpoint layer with the upstream core, tested the checkpoint layer separately, and excluded low-specificity terminal-modification genes from the downstream set. Group summaries reported medians, interquartile ranges, and ranges. Pairwise comparisons used Mann-Whitney U tests with a normal approximation, together with rank-biserial and common-language effect sizes. Because the pathway gene set is small and matched-null controls, expression breadth, essentiality, and other covariates remain incomplete, constraint results were interpreted as evidence that can refine the architecture model, not as proof of robustness or adaptation.

Disease architecture was built in three evidence tiers. The first tier used the GeneReviews Congenital Disorders of N-Linked Glycosylation and Multiple Pathway Overview, accessed on 2026-06-03, as a conservative curated source for known CDG and overlapping multiple-pathway disease genes. `scripts/build_nglyco_disease_seed_table.py` matched GeneReviews CDG genes to the pathway gene table by HGNC symbol and generated the initial disease annotation table and region-level summary. Genes absent from the GeneReviews seed curation were not interpreted as disease-free; they were only classified as not present in this first high-confidence source.

The second disease tier added ClinVar pathogenic and likely pathogenic evidence with `scripts/add_nglyco_clinvar_layer.py`. The local input was `variant_summary.txt.gz`, downloaded from the NCBI ClinVar FTP site on 2026-06-03. The analysis retained GRCh38 germline rows whose clinical significance contained pathogenic or likely pathogenic wording and excluded benign, uncertain, conflicting, not-provided, association, risk-factor, protective, affects, and drug-response labels. Unique VariationID counts and condition counts were summarized by pathway gene. These counts were used as clinical evidence features, not as direct severity scores, because ClinVar volume reflects submission density, gene length, assay history, disease ascertainment, and curation practices.

The third disease and trait tier used the NHGRI-EBI GWAS Catalog v1.0.2 associations archive `e115_r2026-06-01`, accessed on 2026-06-03, via `scripts/add_nglyco_gwas_trait_layer.py`. Associations were retained when reported genes or mapped genes contained one of the curated pathway symbols. Trait text, mapped traits, and study text were classified into broad categories including glycome, immune or inflammation, infection, cancer, tissue identity, and metabolism using explicit keyword rules. GWAS evidence was treated as hypothesis-generating because reported and mapped genes are not equivalent to causal genes. Raw association counts were considered too dense and annotation-dependent to serve as disease-burden scores; category profiles and audited candidate examples were considered more informative.

The disease architecture figure was generated with `scripts/plot_disease_architecture.py` from the region-level disease summary table. The upper layer plotted curated CDG and ClinVar pathogenic or likely pathogenic gene fractions by pathway region, while the lower layer plotted GWAS Catalog trait-category fractions. This separation was deliberate: curated CDG and ClinVar evidence speak to severe Mendelian and pathogenic-variant burden, whereas GWAS Catalog categories speak to broad complex-trait and glycan-output evidence. The figure was therefore designed to compare evidence layers without collapsing them into a single disease score.

Downstream GWAS examples were audited in two steps before being considered for manuscript use. `scripts/audit_downstream_gwas_candidates.py` restricted attention to primary downstream genes in Golgi core processing, Golgi branching, and terminal modification, prioritized direct glycome or glycosylation trait text, and treated reported-gene direct glycome matches as stronger than mapped-gene-only matches. `scripts/review_downstream_gwas_loci.py` then extracted SNP, region, chromosome, position, reported-gene, mapped-gene, context, intergenic status, and nearby-gene fields for strong downstream candidates and assigned a provisional locus-assignment risk label. Even lower-risk rows were treated as association evidence only; causal gene claims require fine mapping, colocalization, coding, eQTL, or functional support beyond this first-pass review.

Interpretation was controlled through a claims register and evidence matrix before manuscript drafting. Claims were classified as demonstrated, supported, consistent with, hypothesis-generating, or not shown. Population-genetic outliers were not treated as demonstrated adaptation. GWAS mapped genes were not treated as causal. Individual candidate genes were used only as examples supporting architecture-level patterns and not as substitutes for region-level evidence. The current analysis is therefore best read as a disciplined, reproducible case study that tests a pathway-architecture model with multiple evidence streams while preserving explicit limits on mechanism, causality, and generalization.

## Results

The curated N-glycosylation pathway table contained 101 genes assigned to seven fine pathway regions and four coarse analysis groups. The main primary-analysis set contained the direct N-glycosylation machinery rather than the broader substrate-support layer. It included 15 lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly genes, 11 oligosaccharyltransferase-transfer genes, 13 primary ER quality-control genes, 7 Golgi core-processing genes, 5 Golgi branching genes, and 16 terminal-modification genes. Thirty substrate-biosynthesis genes were retained for annotation and sensitivity context but were not included in the default upstream-core versus downstream-diversification contrast. This curation produced a pathway architecture in which upstream core, checkpoint, downstream diversification, and substrate support could be tested separately rather than collapsed into a single linear order.

The architecture feature table captured both pathway position and functional priors. All lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly genes and all oligosaccharyltransferase-transfer genes were assigned a high catastrophic-potential prior, as were the ER quality-control genes. In contrast, Golgi core-processing, Golgi branching, and terminal-modification genes carried high or moderate interface-layer priors. Downstream regions were also enriched for paralog-family structure: all Golgi core-processing and Golgi branching genes belonged to a paralog family in the first-pass annotation, and 10 of 16 terminal-modification genes were flagged as low-specificity terminal enzymes. These features support treating N-glycosylation as a structured architecture rather than as an unordered gene set, while also making clear that substrate-support and low-specificity terminal genes require sensitivity handling.

The provisional gnomAD v4.1 constraint analysis did not support the simplest prediction that upstream-core genes are uniformly more coding-constrained than downstream-diversification genes. Constraint metrics were available for 95 of 101 genes. In the primary contrast, upstream-core genes had a higher median LOEUF than downstream-diversification genes, with medians of 0.909 and 0.740, respectively. Because lower LOEUF indicates stronger loss-of-function constraint, this direction is opposite to a naive upstream-more-constrained model. The rank-based comparison was consistent with that direction, with a median difference of 0.169 and a two-sided normal-approximation p value of 0.010. Missense constraint showed a similar complication: downstream-diversification genes had higher median missense Z than upstream-core genes, with medians of 1.4363 and 0.8502, respectively.

Sensitivity analyses refined rather than removed this pattern. When the checkpoint layer was merged with the upstream core, the median LOEUF remained higher than in downstream-diversification genes, although the contrast weakened and the p value rose to 0.131. When the checkpoint layer was tested separately, its median LOEUF was 0.685, close to and slightly lower than the downstream-diversification median of 0.740, but the comparison was not statistically strong in this small gene set. Excluding low-specificity terminal-modification genes from the downstream set did not reverse the primary LOEUF direction: upstream-core genes still had median LOEUF 0.909 compared with 0.740 in the reduced downstream set.

To investigate whether the LOEUF inversion was explained by gene-level compositional differences, we retrieved Ensembl paralog counts and Human Protein Atlas GTEx expression breadth (number of tissues with nTPM ≥ 1) for all 101 genes, and used genomic span as a proxy for gene length. Two structural asymmetries between groups were immediately apparent. First, downstream-diversification genes have substantially longer genomic spans than upstream-core genes, with medians of 71 kb and 48 kb, respectively. CDS length was the strongest predictor of LOEUF in Spearman correlation across all primary-analysis genes (rho = −0.53, p < 0.001), consistent with the known property that LOEUF = observed/expected LoF is partially determined by gene length: longer genes have more expected LoF variants, lowering the LOEUF denominator independently of functional importance. In OLS regression, adding log gene length to the group model reduced the downstream coefficient from −0.20 (p = 0.056) to −0.13 (p = 0.22), indicating that the group difference is substantially explained by the differential gene length between pathway regions. Expression breadth was nearly identical across groups — both upstream and downstream genes are expressed across all or nearly all GTEx tissues — and did not explain the pattern.

Second, downstream-diversification genes have dramatically more Ensembl paralogs than upstream-core genes, with medians of 6 and 1, respectively. This 6-fold difference is the most striking compositional asymmetry between pathway regions. However, paralog count did not correlate significantly with LOEUF in this gene set (rho = −0.18, p = 0.21), indicating that downstream paralogs have not led to relaxed coding constraint through redundancy. Instead, the downstream paralogs represent diversified gene families — the B4GALT1–6 galactosyltransferases, the MAN1A1/MAN1A2/MAN1C1 mannosidases, the ST3GAL and ST6GAL sialyltransferases — whose members have distinct substrate preferences, acceptor specificities, and tissue-expression profiles. The downstream layer has not gained functional redundancy; it has expanded functional range through paralog diversification.

These results argue against using pathway depth alone as the constraint signal and argue that the LOEUF inversion is partly a technical artifact of gene length. They suggest instead that coding constraint in the downstream layer localizes to specific high-output nodes and that the distinctive feature of the downstream region is gene-family diversification rather than coding dispensability. Strong loss-of-function constraint at individual downstream nodes was observed for `MGAT5` (LOEUF 0.415, genomic span 335 kb) and `MAN1A1` (LOEUF 0.512), consistent with specific functional importance at the rate-limiting branching step and early Golgi mannosidase step, respectively, rather than a broad downstream tolerance for disruption.

Disease annotations provided stronger support for a layered architecture. Curated CDG evidence was concentrated in early shared machinery. Lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly had curated CDG evidence for 11 of 15 genes, corresponding to a CDG seed fraction of 0.733. Oligosaccharyltransferase transfer had curated CDG evidence for 5 of 11 genes, corresponding to a fraction of 0.455. ClinVar pathogenic or likely pathogenic evidence was also high in lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, where 14 of 15 genes had at least one filtered germline P/LP variant, and in substrate biosynthesis, where 23 of 30 genes had P/LP evidence. These results are consistent with the idea that early shared precursor and transfer machinery has high clinical sensitivity, although substrate support remains analytically distinct from the direct upstream-core contrast.

Downstream-diversification regions showed weaker severe Mendelian/CDG burden. Golgi branching had no curated CDG genes and no ClinVar P/LP genes in the first-pass table. Golgi core processing had 1 curated CDG gene among 7 genes and 2 genes with ClinVar P/LP evidence. Terminal modification had 1 curated CDG gene among 16 genes and 3 genes with ClinVar P/LP evidence. The correct interpretation is not that downstream genes lack disease relevance; several downstream genes have known or candidate clinical and trait associations. Rather, the severe Mendelian/CDG layer was not evenly distributed across the pathway and was strongest in lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, and substrate-support machinery.

GWAS Catalog evidence showed a different pattern from curated CDG evidence. GWAS annotations were dense across the pathway, with nearly every pathway gene having at least one reported or mapped association. This made raw GWAS counts poor burden scores. However, trait-category summaries and downstream candidate audits were informative. Across the downstream-diversification regions, 9 genes had glycome-category GWAS evidence and 22 had immune or inflammation-category evidence. Golgi branching was especially glycome-rich, with 4 of 5 genes carrying glycome-category evidence, while terminal-modification genes showed broad interface-category coverage, including glycome evidence in 5 of 16 genes and immune or inflammation evidence in 14 of 16 genes. These data support a descriptive downstream trait-profile signal, with the important caveat that GWAS Catalog gene annotations are not causal assignments.

The downstream GWAS candidate audit identified seven strong downstream candidates for cautious manuscript examples: `FUT8`, `ST6GAL1`, `MGAT3`, `B4GALT1`, `ST3GAL4`, `FUT3`, and `MGAT5`. These genes were prioritized because they had direct glycome or glycosylation trait text and varying levels of reported-gene support. The subsequent locus review showed that `ST6GAL1` was the cleanest current example, with 79 direct glycome locus rows, 39 reported-gene rows, and no higher-risk rows under the current triage rule. `MGAT3` also had extensive direct glycome evidence, with 91 direct glycome rows and 28 reported-gene rows, but 40 higher-risk rows required assignment caution. `B4GALT1` linked downstream enzymatic function, curated CDG status, ClinVar P/LP evidence, and 67 direct glycome rows, making it useful as a mixed Mendelian-and-glycome example rather than as a purely downstream-tolerant gene.

Other downstream examples were informative but required stronger caveats. `FUT8` had the largest number of direct glycome rows, 203, and 38 reported-gene rows, but many rows were moderate or higher risk under the locus-assignment triage. `MGAT5` had 16 direct glycome rows and 4 reported-gene rows, supporting its use as a branching-enzyme example. `ST3GAL4` and `FUT3` had direct glycome or glycosylation evidence but weaker or more complex locus-assignment profiles. These examples are best used to illustrate the downstream glycome-output and interface trait layer, not to claim adaptation or causal gene assignment from GWAS annotation alone.

Taken together, the first-pass results support a refined version of the architecture model. The simple coding-constraint gradient is not supported: upstream-core genes were not more loss-of-function constrained than downstream-diversification genes in the provisional gnomAD v4.1 analysis. The stronger current pattern is layered. Severe Mendelian and pathogenic-variant evidence is concentrated in early shared machinery, especially lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, and substrate-support genes. Downstream branching and terminal-modification regions show weaker severe CDG burden but richer glycome and interface trait-category evidence. The current evidence therefore supports a model in which robustness and evolvability are not separated by coding constraint alone, but by the combination of disease architecture, glycan-output variation, and context-dependent trait associations across pathway regions.

## Discussion

The primary result of this study is that N-glycosylation's evolutionary architecture is layered in a way that is visible in disease burden and trait association profiles, but is not captured by a simple coding-constraint gradient. Early shared machinery — lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, and substrate-support genes — concentrates curated CDG disease evidence and pathogenic ClinVar variants. Downstream branching and terminal-modification regions carry much weaker severe Mendelian burden, but richer glycome and immune or interface trait-category evidence from the GWAS Catalog. This separation is consistent with a biological architecture in which functions that would be catastrophic if disrupted are placed early in a shared core, while downstream combinatorial processing and organism-environment output functions are more tolerant of variation in their severe disease consequences and more connected to adaptive or quantitative trait biology.

### Coding constraint does not cleanly separate the pathway

The provisional gnomAD v4.1 constraint analysis was deliberately treated as a complicating rather than a confirming result. Upstream-core genes had a higher median LOEUF than downstream-diversification genes, meaning that by this metric, upstream genes were less loss-of-function constrained. Missense Z showed a similar reversal. Sensitivity analyses showed that these patterns persisted when the checkpoint layer was merged with the upstream core and when low-specificity terminal-modification genes were removed from the downstream set. These results argue against the simplest version of the architecture hypothesis, in which upstream position maps monotonically to stronger coding intolerance.

Covariate regression identified gene length as the primary driver of the LOEUF inversion. Downstream-diversification genes have longer genomic spans than upstream-core genes (medians 71 kb vs 48 kb), and CDS length is the strongest LOEUF predictor across all primary-analysis genes (Spearman rho = −0.53, p < 0.001). Because LOEUF is computed as observed divided by expected LoF variants, and expected counts scale with CDS length, longer genes mechanically tend toward lower LOEUF scores regardless of their functional importance. Adding log gene length to the regression reduced the pathway-group coefficient from −0.20 (p = 0.056) to −0.13 (p = 0.22). The LOEUF inversion is therefore substantially a gene-length artifact rather than a biological signal of downstream coding constraint. Expression breadth was uninformative — both groups are expressed across virtually all GTEx tissues — while paralog count differed dramatically between groups (downstream median 6, upstream median 1) but did not itself predict LOEUF.

The paralog asymmetry is the most biologically interesting structural difference between pathway regions. Upstream-core genes are predominantly singletons or have one close paralog; their functions are non-redundant, and loss typically causes catastrophic disruption of shared glycoprotein processing. Downstream-diversification genes belong to large enzyme families — B4GALT, MAN1, ST3GAL, ST6GAL, MGAT4, CHST — whose members have diverged in substrate preference, acceptor specificity, and tissue context. This is gene-family diversification rather than redundancy: the paralogs are not backup copies but functionally distinct variants covering different glycan outputs and biological contexts. The downstream layer has therefore accumulated evolvable functional range, which is consistent with the robustness/evolvability model even though coding constraint does not cleanly separate the pathway [montanucci_primate_nglycosylation_network].

The most constrained downstream genes — `MGAT5` (LOEUF 0.415), `MAN1A1` (LOEUF 0.512), `MAN1A2` (LOEUF 0.525) — are rate-limiting or high-output nodes within the downstream layer, consistent with functional specificity and network centrality driving constraint at specific positions rather than a uniform downstream tolerance. Taken together, the constraint results are best interpreted as follows: the LOEUF inversion is a gene-length confound; the real structural distinction between upstream and downstream is paralog diversification versus singularity; and the downstream layer expresses evolvability through gene-family expansion and functional range, not through coding dispensability.

### Disease architecture provides stronger architecture support than constraint alone

The disease and trait evidence provides more direct support for the layered architecture model than the constraint analysis. Congenital disorders of glycosylation arise overwhelmingly from disruption of shared early machinery. Eleven of fifteen lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly genes, five of eleven oligosaccharyltransferase genes, and the bulk of substrate-support donors have curated CDG or pathogenic ClinVar evidence. This pattern is expected if these early steps are catastrophic nodes whose disruption affects broad glycoprotein maturation rather than the output of any particular downstream branch. Downstream branching and terminal-modification genes had much weaker CDG evidence, with zero curated CDG genes in the Golgi branching group and one each in Golgi core processing and terminal modification.

This disease burden separation supports the catastrophic-core hypothesis more directly than constraint alone because clinical evidence integrates severity, mechanism, and human phenotypic outcome in a way that population-genomic metrics cannot. It is also worth noting that the disease evidence is conservative: the GeneReviews CDG seed table is curated and not exhaustive, and ClinVar evidence reflects ascertainment and submission history. The absence of CDG evidence for most downstream genes does not mean those genes are clinically irrelevant. `B4GALT1` is a useful illustration: it has curated CDG annotation and ClinVar pathogenic variants alongside extensive glycome GWAS evidence, making it an example of a downstream gene with both disease and glycan-output signals. The correct interpretation is therefore not that downstream genes are safe from disease, but that severe Mendelian disruption concentrates in shared early steps, while downstream perturbation is more likely to manifest as quantitative trait or context-dependent phenotypic variation.

### Downstream glycome and interface trait evidence supports a qualitatively different evolutionary regime

GWAS Catalog evidence was dense and not directly interpretable as a burden score. The informative signal was in the trait-category profile rather than raw association counts. Downstream branching and terminal-modification regions were substantially enriched for glycome-category and immune or inflammation-category evidence relative to upstream-core genes. Among the five Golgi branching genes, four had glycome-category GWAS evidence. Among the sixteen terminal-modification genes, fourteen had immune or inflammation-category evidence. This profile is consistent with a picture in which downstream glycan output is a site of tolerated variation that propagates into quantitative phenotypic differences in glycan structures, immune cell function, inflammatory responses, and organism-environment interactions [varki_golgi_evolution; marth_grewal_immunity; rabinovich_glycan_immunity].

The strongest individual downstream examples for cautious manuscript use are `ST6GAL1` and `MGAT3`, each with large numbers of direct glycome locus rows and substantial reported-gene support in the GWAS Catalog. `FUT8` has the largest raw glycome locus count but requires more careful assignment caution because many rows involve intergenic or distal mapped-gene associations. `MGAT5` is a useful branching-enzyme example with more modest but direct glycome evidence. These examples illustrate the interface-trait profile of downstream genes without claiming causal assignment from GWAS annotation.

It is important to note what this evidence does and does not show. GWAS-reported or -mapped gene assignments are not causal identifications. The downstream glycome and trait signal could partly reflect gene density, LD structure, or proximity to other genes in densely annotated chromosomal regions. The trait-category enrichment observed here is descriptive. It motivates follow-up with fine mapping, colocalization, functional and eQTL evidence, and replicated population-based glycan phenotyping studies, but it does not on its own constitute a demonstration of downstream-layer evolvability.

### Cross-species conservation complements rather than extends the constraint result

Cross-species ortholog % identity was flat across pathway regions in the primary upstream-core versus downstream-diversification contrast, with no statistically meaningful difference in median mouse or chimp % identity between the two groups. This result is consistent with prior primate coding evolution studies, which found that N-glycosylation genes as a group are strongly conserved at the coding sequence level, with network topology rather than pathway depth predicting conservation [montanucci_primate_nglycosylation_network]. The null conservation result is informative: it confirms that the downstream glycan-output layer does not have a uniformly relaxed coding sequence, which is consistent with the view that evolvability in this pathway is expressed through glycan-output variation, regulatory and expression-level control, and context-dependent trait biology rather than through accelerated protein-coding divergence.

Within the OST transfer group, median mouse % identity was highest across all pathway subgroups (~99%), consistent with tight structural requirements for the multi-subunit membrane-embedded catalytic complex. LLO assembly showed somewhat lower median % identity (~86%) despite carrying the strongest CDG disease burden, suggesting that some sequence-level divergence from rodents is compatible with deeply conserved function when the functional architecture is maintained. These within-pathway contrasts are hypothesis-generating and should not be overinterpreted given the small gene-set sizes and the known limitations of % identity as a conservation measure.

### Population-genetic analysis finds no coding-sweep gradient but identifies candidate outlier genes

We computed Weir-Cockerham FST for all ten pairwise superpopulation contrasts and Population Branch Statistics for five population trios, using 1000 Genomes Phase 3 VCF data, for 99 of 101 pathway genes. We also extracted PopHuman iHS scores in 10 kb windows across 26 populations for 96 genes. These analyses were designed as a hypothesis-generating supporting layer, not as the primary evidence for the architecture model.

The main result is negative: there was no statistically significant FST or iHS gradient between upstream-core and downstream-diversification genes in any of the ten pairwise FST contrasts or any of the five superpopulation iHS summaries. For the AFR-EUR contrast — the most powered contrast given sample sizes — upstream-core genes had median per-gene mean FST of 0.081 and downstream-diversification genes had median 0.085, a trivial and statistically non-significant difference. iHS max in AFR showed upstream-core median 1.38 and downstream-diversification median 1.25, also not meaningfully different.

This null gradient is not surprising given the prior literature. Population-genetic sweeps leave signatures primarily in non-coding regulatory regions and in specific populations under specific demographic histories. The current analysis measured per-gene mean SNP-level FST weighted across the gene body, which is not optimized to detect soft sweeps, polygenic shifts, or regulatory variation outside the transcript. Furthermore, both upstream and downstream N-glycosylation genes are under strong purifying constraint at the coding level, limiting the accumulation of differentiated coding variants. The population-genetic analysis therefore does not falsify the evolvability hypothesis; it is consistent with the interpretation that downstream evolvability is expressed through glycan-output variation, expression context, and regulatory channels rather than through differentiating coding sweeps.

Several individual gene outliers merit hypothesis-generating note. `FUT8`, a core-fucosylation terminal-modification enzyme, had the highest iHS max in AFR (3.39) and EAS (3.38) among all pathway genes in the primary analysis set, crossing the conventional soft-sweep threshold of |iHS| > 2 in multiple populations. FUT8 modifies IgG and other secreted glycoproteins in a context with established phenotypic consequences for antibody effector function. MOGS, an ER-resident checkpoint gene at the glycan-processing transition, had the highest mean pairwise FST in the AFR-EUR contrast (0.285), notably elevated above the pathway-wide median of ~0.08. NGLY1, an ER peptide:N-glycanase involved in glycoprotein degradation, also showed elevated AFR differentiation signals. These genes are downstream of or at the checkpoint boundary, consistent in direction with the architecture model, but sample sizes at the gene level are too small to distinguish drift from selection, and the analysis was not corrected for gene length, recombination rate, or demographic structure. These outlier observations are best treated as candidate examples for follow-up rather than as evidence of adaptation.

### Comparator pathway analysis supports the specificity of N-glycosylation's architecture

To ask whether the N-glycosylation constraint pattern is distinctive rather than a generic property of metabolic pathway genes, we curated two comparator pathways — heme biosynthesis (9 genes, fully linear, no downstream combinatorial diversity) and coenzyme Q biosynthesis (13 genes, core-sequential plus late-modification structure) — and retrieved gnomAD v4.1 constraint metrics for each.

Heme biosynthesis showed a markedly different constraint pattern from N-glycosylation. Median LOEUF for heme genes was 0.70, below the commonly cited LOEUF < 0.35 strong constraint threshold but substantially lower than the upstream-core N-glycosylation median of 0.91. Crucially, heme constraint was uniform across the pathway: both early and late heme biosynthesis genes had LOEUF near 0.70, with no detectable gradient between pathway positions. This uniformity is expected given heme's biochemistry — the pathway produces a single essential cofactor for hemoproteins and mitochondrial electron transport, with no downstream combinatorial diversification layer. Every step in a strictly linear pathway contributes to the same endpoint and carries similar dependency structure.

CoQ biosynthesis showed the opposite pattern: median LOEUF near 1.0 (both core-sequential and late-modification stages), indicating that CoQ genes are broadly loss-of-function tolerant relative to the genome average. This may partly reflect the availability of dietary CoQ supplementation, the existence of parallel biosynthesis branches, and the smaller clinical ascertainment of CoQ deficiency disorders compared with hemolytic or porphyric disease. CoQ genes had zero constrained-LOEUF (<0.35) genes in the current analysis.

The N-glycosylation pattern contrasts with both comparators in a way that is informative about the architecture model. Unlike heme, N-glycosylation does not show uniform coding constraint; its constraint signal is strongest at dependency and checkpoint nodes across multiple pathway positions. Unlike CoQ, N-glycosylation has a strong disease architecture signal concentrated in specific pathway regions. The distinctive feature of N-glycosylation is therefore not constraint level per se but the combination of concentrated severe Mendelian disease in the early shared core with a rich downstream glycan-output and interface-trait profile — a combination that heme (all disease, no interface-trait diversity) and CoQ (little disease concentration, no interface-trait diversity) both lack. This specificity comparison supports the view that N-glycosylation's layered architecture is a property of its topology, not simply a consequence of its essentiality or expression breadth.

### Relationship to robustness and evolvability theory

The results are most naturally interpreted within the robustness/evolvability framework developed by Kitano, Felix and Wagner, Payne and Wagner, and related work on modular and fault-tolerant biological architectures [kitano_biological_robustness; felix_wagner_pervasive_robustness; payne_wagner_causes_evolvability]. That framework predicts that biological systems will tend to localize catastrophic failure points and protect them from variation, while organizing phenotypic variation to be expressed through interface or output layers. The N-glycosylation results are consistent with this prediction at the disease-architecture level, where severe Mendelian disruption concentrates in early shared machinery while downstream variation is connected to quantitative interface traits.

The results also echo analogies to other biological systems that operate as interface layers. Glycan structures at cell surfaces, in the serum, and at mucosal and epithelial interfaces are contacted by pathogens, immune effectors, lectins, and environmental ligands in ways that place a premium on variation and context-dependence [varki_golgi_evolution; marth_grewal_immunity; rabinovich_glycan_immunity; smith_cummings_virus_host_glycans]. The downstream glycan-output and terminal-modification machinery is in this sense analogous to other fast-varying biological interface systems — immune receptor diversification, olfactory receptor expansion, mucin domain variation — in which combinatorial or diversified output faces selection or drift from the external environment. The N-glycosylation case is notable because it connects a highly conserved shared biosynthetic core to a combinatorially diverse and immunologically active output layer through a well-characterized biochemical pathway.

The comparator pathway results add a specificity argument that the robustness/evolvability framework predicts. A pathway with a strictly linear topology, such as heme biosynthesis, should show uniform robustness without a downstream interface-facing evolvability layer, because there is no combinatorial output or organism-environment contact at the pathway endpoint. A pathway producing a non-diversified essential cofactor, such as CoQ, should show tolerant constraint because dietary supplementation and genetic redundancy buffer coding loss. N-glycosylation's layered disease architecture and downstream interface-trait profile is therefore most naturally explained by its specific topology: a conserved shared core that feeds a combinatorial downstream processing network whose outputs contact immune, infectious, and environmental inputs. Testing whether other pathways with similar topology — GPI-anchor biosynthesis, or O-glycosylation — show comparable layered architecture would allow this prediction to be evaluated beyond the single-system level.

### Limitations

Several limitations restrict the strength of inferences drawn from the current analysis.

**Pathway curation and gene classification.** The gene table was assembled from Reactome release 96 supplemented by textbook-level knowledge of N-glycan biosynthesis. Some gene assignments are provisional: Ensembl IDs and coordinates were drawn from MyGene.info without complete Ensembl and HGNC cross-checking; some downstream terminal-modification genes are of low substrate specificity and were flagged for sensitivity handling; and a few genes with multi-role or ambiguous assignments have not been resolved. The upstream/downstream binary, while motivated by the architecture question, is an approximation of a biochemical continuum. Sensitivity analyses varying the boundary between checkpoint, upstream, and downstream groups were conducted but did not remove the primary disease-architecture result.

**Constraint metrics are provisional and not covariate-controlled.** The gnomAD constraint analysis used a provisional local file and has not yet been cross-validated against the full v4.1.1 release. More critically, matched null controls, gene-length correction, recombination-rate covariate adjustment, and expression-breadth or essentiality covariates have not been implemented. These covariates matter because pathway genes may systematically differ from genome-wide background in expression breadth, essentiality, GC content, and local genomic context. The current constraint results are therefore exploratory and should not be cited as evidence of a demonstrated constraint gradient.

**GWAS annotations are not causal gene assignments.** The GWAS Catalog reported-gene and mapped-gene columns were used as evidence features and explicitly labeled as hypothesis-generating. The glycome and interface trait profile observed downstream is descriptive. It is compatible with the architecture hypothesis, but the same pattern could arise from gene density, LD extent, population-specific ascertainment, or proximity to other genes with primary disease roles. Fine mapping, colocalization with cis-eQTLs, coding variant analysis, and targeted functional studies would be needed to substantiate causal claims for individual loci.

**Cross-species conservation is incomplete.** The PhyloP100 fetch returned complete data for approximately half the gene set due to intermittent network errors during the UCSC API run. Conservation % identity and GOC score are descriptive proxies that do not measure selective pressure direction. dN/dS ratios are no longer available from the Ensembl REST API or BioMart in recent releases, which limits the ability to distinguish fast-evolving from slow-evolving genes using classical molecular-evolution metrics.

**Sample sizes are small and multiple comparisons are uncontrolled.** The primary contrast involves approximately 25 upstream-core and 26 downstream-diversification genes. Mann-Whitney tests in samples of this size have limited power, and the multiple comparisons across contrasts and metrics were not formally corrected. Effect sizes and confidence intervals were reported to quantify uncertainty, but statistical results should be interpreted with appropriate caution and not treated as genome-wide calibrated p-values.

**Population-genetic analyses are hypothesis-generating and uncontrolled for neutral demography.** FST and iHS analyses were conducted using 1000 Genomes Phase 3 data and PopHuman pre-computed statistics. The primary gradient test found no statistically significant pathway-position effect. Individual gene outliers (FUT8, MOGS, NGLY1) are noted as hypothesis-generating candidates. However, the analyses were not corrected for gene length, recombination rate, local GC content, or demographic history. Per-gene mean FST captures overall differentiation but misses soft sweeps, regulatory selection, or signals restricted to specific haplotypes. iHS from pre-computed 10 kb windows cannot resolve signals within gene bodies or distinguish selection from background LD patterns. Population-genetic results should therefore be treated strictly as exploratory context for the architecture model, not as evidence of population-specific adaptation.

**Comparator pathways are few and not matched on expression breadth or essentiality.** Heme biosynthesis (9 genes) and CoQ biosynthesis (13 genes) were chosen as structurally contrasting comparators: strictly linear, without a downstream combinatorial-diversity layer. The constraint comparison with N-glycosylation is informative in direction, but expression breadth, tissue specificity, gene length, recombination rate, and essentiality covariates were not controlled across pathway sets. The comparator analysis establishes that N-glycosylation's constraint pattern is not simply replicated in these reference pathways; it does not demonstrate that the difference is caused by pathway topology specifically.

**Generalizability beyond N-glycosylation is untested.** The architecture hypothesis is presented as a case study, not as a universal principle. Whether other biosynthetic pathways with a core-plus-interface topology — GPI-anchor biosynthesis, O-glycosylation, or complex lipid biosynthesis — show similar layered disease and trait profiles has not been examined. The comparator pathways included here were chosen to contrast the N-glyco architecture, not to sample the full space of possible pathway topologies.

### Conclusions

N-glycosylation is a useful case study for testing whether biological pathways partition catastrophic core functions from interface-facing evolvable outputs. The strongest current support for this architecture comes from disease evidence: severe Mendelian and CDG disease burden is concentrated in lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, and substrate-support machinery, while downstream branching and terminal-modification regions carry much weaker severe disease burden and richer glycome and immune or interface trait-category associations. Coding constraint and cross-species conservation do not support a simple linear gradient from conserved upstream to tolerant downstream, but they are consistent with a more structured picture in which robustness is distributed across dependency, checkpoint, and structural nodes rather than concentrated by pathway depth. Population-genetic FST and iHS analyses found no significant upstream-to-downstream gradient, reinforcing the interpretation that downstream evolvability operates through glycan-output variation and regulatory channels rather than through differentiating coding sweeps. Comparator pathway analysis confirmed that the layered pattern is distinctive to N-glycosylation's topology: heme biosynthesis shows uniform constraint without interface-trait diversity, and CoQ biosynthesis shows uniform tolerance without concentrated disease architecture.

The results argue against two extreme models: the model in which upstream genes are simply constrained and downstream genes are simply adaptive, and the model in which N-glycosylation is architecturally uniform. The evidence supports a view in which the pathway contains biologically distinct zones, identifiable by disease architecture and glycan-output trait profiles, even though protein-coding conservation and population differentiation metrics do not cleanly separate them. This view motivates continued work on regulatory architecture, glycan-output phenotyping, topology-matched comparator pathways, and formal quantitative modeling of pathway robustness and evolvability.
