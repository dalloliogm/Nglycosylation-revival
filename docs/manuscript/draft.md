# Manuscript Draft

Working title: Robustness and evolvability across a glycosylation pathway: N-glycosylation as a case study in evolutionary architecture

Date started: 2026-06-06

Draft status: first-pass Introduction, Methods, and Results. Claims and wording should be checked against `docs/manuscript/evidence-matrix.md` before expanding Discussion.

## Abstract

Biological pathways must simultaneously preserve functions whose disruption is lethal and generate phenotypic variation accessible to selection. Whether robustness and evolvability are spatially organized within a single pathway — concentrated in different gene sets — is an open question. We test this using N-glycosylation, a pathway with a conserved biosynthetic core and a combinatorially diverse glycan-output layer.

We curated 101 N-glycosylation genes and evaluated six evidence streams. Severe CDG and Mendelian disease burden concentrated in upstream-core genes (73% of lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly genes) and was low in downstream branching regions (0% Golgi branching in the curated CDG layer). A genome-wide GWAS null model supported layer-specific enrichment for glycan and immune traits downstream (downstream vs. upstream OR = 4.24), while hit-count matching weakened the genome-wide contrast, indicating substantial annotation and gene-density confounding. IgG glycan locus partitioning showed a striking downstream-skewed pattern: all seven N-glyco genes assigned independent IgG glycan loci are downstream, zero are upstream (OR = ∞, p = 0.003), but these remain GWAS Catalog gene assignments rather than causal proofs. The apparent LOEUF constraint inversion is consistent with gene-length confounding and a plausible survivor-sampling bias in recessive lethal CDG genes. Comparator analyses suggest that the disease gradient is topology-dependent, with a similar pattern in heparan sulfate biosynthesis and less separation in linear pathways. Pairwise human–mouse dN/dS was indistinguishable across layers (medians 0.075 vs. 0.073, p = 0.49), supporting the view that downstream evolvability, if present, is more visible through glycan-output and regulatory variation than through accelerated coding evolution.

These results support a model in which N-glycosylation's topology contributes to a spatial partition of robustness and evolvability. The downstream layer is not simply weakly constrained; it is where current glycan-output trait associations concentrate. Whether this architecture generalizes to other pathways is a testable prediction of the model.

## Introduction

Biological systems must solve a recurrent architectural problem. They must preserve functions whose failure would compromise viability, while retaining enough flexibility to generate phenotypic variation in changing environments. Robustness and evolvability are therefore often discussed as system-level properties rather than attributes of single genes. Robustness can arise from buffering, modularity, feedback, degeneracy, and network organization, while evolvability depends on the capacity to generate heritable phenotypic variation that can be exposed, filtered, or reused under new conditions [1,2,3,4]. A central unresolved question is whether these properties are spatially organized within biological pathways. If a pathway contains both catastrophic core functions and environmentally exposed output functions, then robustness and evolvability may be localized to different regions of the same system.

N-glycosylation provides a tractable case study for this question because it has a strongly ordered biochemical architecture and a biologically diverse output layer. Early steps construct a lipid-linked oligosaccharide precursor, transfer it to nascent proteins through the oligosaccharyltransferase machinery, and connect glycan processing to endoplasmic reticulum folding and quality-control systems. These steps affect broad glycoprotein maturation and therefore have plausible catastrophic-failure potential [5,6,7]. Later Golgi processing, branching, and terminal modification generate diverse mature glycan structures whose consequences are often deployed at protein surfaces, cell surfaces, secreted proteins, and tissue-specific contexts. This downstream layer is biologically connected to immune regulation, host-pathogen interaction, inflammation, cancer biology, mucosal interfaces, and glycan-mediated cell communication [8,9,10,11,12,13].

This architecture motivates a testable model: N-glycosylation may preserve robustness in core and checkpoint-linked machinery while exposing evolvability through downstream glycan-output, regulatory, tissue-specific, and organism-environment interface layers. The model does not require downstream genes to be weakly constrained in protein-coding sequence, nor does it require every downstream association to represent local adaptation. Prior work already argues against such a simple view. Molecular-evolution and network studies of N-glycosylation found strong conservation and network-associated purifying constraint, while glyco-regulation work emphasized that glycan phenotypes arise from regulated networks of glyco-genes rather than from one-to-one template encoding [14,15,16,17]. In this framing, coding conservation and evolvability are not opposites. Evolvability may be expressed through glycan-output variation, regulatory control, expression context, and downstream trait effects even when many enzymes remain under coding constraint.

The historical starting point for this project is the 2012 analysis of positive selection and population differentiation in the asparagine N-glycosylation pathway [18]. That study raised the possibility that evolutionary signals are unevenly distributed across pathway regions, but its population-genetic evidence is not a sufficient foundation for the modern argument. Older SNP-array resources, continental groupings, one-versus-rest differentiation summaries, fixed windows around genes, linkage disequilibrium, and demographic confounding all limit causal interpretation. The stronger modern contribution is therefore not to reproduce every original statistic. Instead, the original analysis is treated as historical context and a hypothesis generator for a broader architecture test.

Here, we formalize N-glycosylation as a pathway-level case study of robustness and evolvability. We curate a modern gene and pathway-edge representation, classify genes into biologically motivated pathway regions, and evaluate whether architecture predicts multiple evidence layers: gene-level constraint, severe Mendelian and congenital-disorder burden, ClinVar pathogenic variation, glycome and complex-trait associations, and downstream interface-related signals. The primary claim is deliberately cautious. We ask whether N-glycosylation supports a layered architecture in which severe disease evidence concentrates in shared core machinery while downstream branching and terminal-modification regions are richer in glycan-output and interface trait evidence. We do not claim that pathway position proves adaptation, that GWAS locus annotations are causal, or that N-glycosylation establishes a universal biological design principle.

The architecture model makes four predictions that can be checked against independent data and that distinguish it from a null model in which pathway position has no systematic effect. First, disruption of upstream-core genes should cause qualitatively more severe human disease than disruption of downstream-diversification genes; a null result — uniform CDG burden across regions — would argue against a catastrophic-core architecture. Second, downstream genes should be disproportionately associated with quantitative glycan-output phenotypes and organismal-interface traits relative to upstream genes, after controlling for gene count and expression breadth; a null result here would argue that any disease-burden gradient is simply an essentiality artifact rather than an interface-layer signal. Third, coding-sequence constraint, conservation, and population-differentiation statistics should not show a simple upstream-more-constrained gradient, because the robustness/evolvability model specifically predicts that evolvability operates through regulatory and output channels rather than coding tolerance; a strong coding gradient would suggest simpler explanations suffice. Fourth, if N-glycosylation's layered architecture is a property of its specific topology — conserved shared core feeding a combinatorial interface-output layer — then pathways lacking that topology (strictly linear pathways, single-product pathways) should not show the same disease-burden and trait-profile separation; a universal pattern across all pathway types would reduce the finding to a trivial consequence of shared-step essentiality. This study tests all four predictions and treats confirmation of each as evidence for the architecture model and falsification of any as evidence requiring model revision.

## Methods

Full methods are provided in Supplementary Methods. Briefly, we curated 101 N-glycosylation genes from Reactome (release 96, event R-HSA-446203) and classified them into seven fine pathway regions and four coarse groups: upstream core (lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, OST transfer, ER quality control), checkpoint, downstream diversification (Golgi branching and terminal modification), and substrate support. Gene-level coding constraint used gnomAD v4.1 LOEUF and missense Z. LOEUF regression covariates (log genomic span, log Ensembl paralog count, GTEx expression breadth) were fitted with OLS in statsmodels. Disease burden used GeneReviews CDG curation and ClinVar P/LP germline variants (accessed 2026-06-03). Comparator pathway disease gradients were assessed by literature curation for GPI-anchor, purine de novo, heparan sulfate, and ceramide/GSL biosynthesis, with Fisher's exact test comparing core vs. downstream Mendelian disease fractions. The genome-wide GWAS null model used all 982,984 GWS associations from GWAS Catalog v1.0.2 (e115_r2026-06-01), classifying traits by keyword and comparing pathway genes to decile-matched background genes. Pairwise dN/dS (NG86 method) used human–mouse canonical CDS pairs retrieved via Ensembl REST, aligned with protein-guided codon alignment (BLOSUM62, global, gap-open −10) to correct signal-peptide misalignment artifacts; 98 of 101 pairs passed a dS ≤ 1.5 saturation filter. IgG glycan locus partitioning used 656 GWS IgG glycan associations from GWAS Catalog, clumped to 54 independent loci by greedy 500 kb windows, and assigned to pathway genes by HGNC symbol matching. All scripts and result tables are available in the project repository.


## Results

The curated N-glycosylation pathway contained 101 genes in seven fine regions: 15 lipid-linked oligosaccharide (LLO) assembly genes, 11 oligosaccharyltransferase (OST) transfer genes, 13 ER quality-control genes, 7 Golgi core-processing genes, 5 Golgi branching genes, 16 terminal-modification genes, and 30 substrate-biosynthesis genes (retained for sensitivity context). The default contrast was upstream core (LLO assembly + OST transfer + ER QC) versus downstream diversification (Golgi branching + terminal modification).

Coding constraint did not show the predicted upstream-more-constrained gradient. Upstream-core genes had *higher* median LOEUF (0.909) than downstream-diversification genes (0.740; Mann-Whitney p = 0.010), the opposite direction of the naive architecture prediction. Downstream genes also had higher median missense Z (1.44 vs. 0.85). OLS regression suggested that gene-length confounding contributes to this inversion: CDS length was the strongest LOEUF predictor (Spearman ρ = −0.53, p < 0.001), and adding log gene length reduced the downstream coefficient from −0.20 (p = 0.056) to −0.13 (p = 0.22). Downstream genes have longer median genomic spans (71 kb vs. 48 kb). Downstream genes also have dramatically more paralogs (medians 6 vs. 1), but these are functionally diversified family members — B4GALT1–6, ST3GAL/ST6GAL sialyltransferases, MAN1A1/A2/C1 mannosidases — not redundant copies.

Severe disease burden was concentrated upstream. LLO assembly had CDG evidence for 11 of 15 genes (73%) and ClinVar P/LP evidence for 14 of 15. OST transfer had CDG evidence for 5 of 11 (45%). Golgi branching had 0 CDG genes and 0 ClinVar P/LP genes; terminal modification had 1 CDG gene (6%) and 3 ClinVar P/LP genes. Comparator pathway analyses suggest that this gradient depends on topology rather than on pathway membership alone: heparan sulfate biosynthesis, which shares N-glycosylation's layered architecture, showed the same direction of separation, with all 8 curated core genes causing severe Mendelian disease and 0 of 16 downstream modification enzymes doing so (Fisher p < 0.0001). Linear pathways in the current comparison showed less evidence of such a gradient.

The genome-wide GWAS null model (982,984 GWS associations; 31,847 background genes) showed that downstream N-glyco genes are enriched for glycan and immune traits relative to upstream N-glyco genes (OR = 4.24) and genome-wide background (OR = 2.47, p = 0.003 unmatched; weakens to OR = 1.63, p = 0.107 after hit-count matching). IgG glycan locus partitioning gave a sharper trait-layer result: of 54 independent IgG glycan GWAS loci (500 kb clumping), 7 were assigned to downstream N-glyco genes (FUT8, MGAT3, ST6GAL1, B4GALT1, FUT3, MGAT5, ST3GAL4; mean |β| 0.25–0.52) and zero were assigned to upstream N-glyco genes (Fisher OR = ∞, p = 0.003). Pairwise human–mouse dN/dS showed no coding-evolution gradient: both layers have indistinguishable median dN/dS (upstream 0.075, downstream 0.073; MW p = 0.49; 98 gene pairs, protein-guided alignment, NG86 method).

## Discussion

The primary result of this study is that N-glycosylation's evolutionary architecture is layered in a way that is visible in disease burden and trait association profiles, but is not captured by a simple coding-constraint gradient. Early shared machinery — lipid-linked oligosaccharide assembly, oligosaccharyltransferase transfer, and substrate-support genes — concentrates curated CDG disease evidence and pathogenic ClinVar variants. Downstream branching and terminal-modification regions carry much weaker severe Mendelian burden, but richer glycome and immune or interface trait-category evidence from the GWAS Catalog. This separation is consistent with a biological architecture in which functions that would be catastrophic if disrupted are placed early in a shared core, while downstream combinatorial processing and organism-environment output functions are more tolerant of variation in their severe disease consequences and more connected to adaptive or quantitative trait biology.

### Coding constraint: the LOEUF inversion reflects both gene-length confounding and a Wald survivor-sampling bias

The most obvious alternative interpretation of the constraint data is that it falsifies the architecture model entirely. Upstream-core genes had higher median LOEUF (0.91) than downstream-diversification genes (0.74), meaning downstream genes appear more constrained. Under this reading, the pathway does not have a tolerant downstream output layer — it has a highly constrained one — and the evolvability claim should be abandoned. Missense Z showed the same reversal.

The data point to two mechanisms, both of which explain the inversion without requiring downstream genes to be genuinely more essential.

The first mechanism is **gene-length confounding**. Downstream-diversification genes have longer genomic spans (medians 71 kb vs. 48 kb), and CDS length is the strongest LOEUF predictor across primary-analysis genes (Spearman ρ = −0.53, p < 0.001). Because LOEUF is computed as observed LoF variants divided by expected LoF variants, and expected counts scale with CDS length, longer genes mechanically tend toward lower (more constrained-looking) LOEUF regardless of fitness consequence. Adding log gene length to the regression reduced the pathway-group coefficient from −0.20 (p = 0.056) to −0.13 (p = 0.22), substantially explaining the inversion.

The second mechanism is a **Wald survivor-sampling bias**. gnomAD is a cohort of living adults — a survivor population. Most N-glyco upstream core CDG genes cause disease via recessive inheritance: PMM2-CDG, ALG1-CDG, ALG2-CDG, ALG3-CDG, and related disorders are caused by homozygous or compound heterozygous LoF. Heterozygous carriers are healthy and appear normally in gnomAD; homozygous null individuals are severely ill or die before adulthood and are absent. gnomAD therefore samples only the carrier distribution for these genes, not the lethal homozygous genotype. The expected count under the LOEUF null model is based on neutral mutation rate, not on the subset of variants compatible with survival to adulthood. This can make recessive lethal genes look more tolerant in LOEUF if only the survivable part of their LoF spectrum is observed. The analogy to Abraham Wald is useful, but it remains an interpretation of ascertainment rather than a direct measurement of missing genotypes.

The current data are compatible with this interpretation. Several severe recessive CDG genes have higher LOEUF than CDS-length-nearby comparators: PMM2 (CDS 738 bp, LOEUF 1.937) versus ALG14 (CDS 648 bp, LOEUF 1.055); ALG1 (CDS 1392 bp, LOEUF 1.419) versus ALG10 (CDS 1419 bp, LOEUF 0.901); ALG2 (CDS 1248 bp, LOEUF 1.408) versus DDOST (CDS 1317 bp, LOEUF 0.747). By contrast, milder CDG genes where patients may survive longer (ALG6, ALG8, ALG11, ALG12) show LOEUF closer to their non-CDG CDS-matched comparators. This pattern is consistent with gnomAD missing some of the most severe allele classes, but it should be treated as an interpretation requiring family-level, genotype-specific, or functional validation. The dominant haploinsufficient OST complex subunits STT3A (LOEUF 0.51) and STT3B (0.48), where even heterozygous LoF can be deleterious, are more clearly visible to gene-level constraint metrics.

This reframes the LOEUF inversion as a warning about what gene-level constraint can and cannot measure in this setting. Some upstream genes that look relatively tolerant by LOEUF may not be biologically tolerant; they may instead include recessive disease genes for which the most damaging genotypes are absent from the survivor cohorts used to estimate constraint. This does not by itself prove the architecture model, but it explains why disease architecture and glycan-output traits may be more informative than LOEUF alone.

The real biological signal is the paralog count asymmetry, which is unaffected by both confounders. Downstream-diversification genes belong to large enzyme families — B4GALT1–6, MAN1A1/A2/C1, ST3GAL, ST6GAL, MGAT4A/B/C, CHST8/10 — with a median of 6 Ensembl paralogs versus 1 for upstream-core genes. These are functionally distinct family members with divergent substrate preferences and tissue contexts, representing gene-family diversification rather than redundancy.

Pairwise human–mouse dN/dS (98 gene pairs, protein-guided codon alignment, NG86, dS ≤ 1.5) supports this more cautious picture: both layers are under deep uniform purifying selection (medians 0.075 vs. 0.073, MW p = 0.49). The most constrained genes are the dominant-essential OST subunits STT3A (dN/dS = 0.006), DAD1 (0.000), and RPN1 (0.039); cross-species % identity is similarly flat. Evolvability in the downstream layer is therefore not evident as accelerated protein-coding evolution in this pairwise analysis, and is more plausibly expressed through glycan-output variation, enzyme-family expansion, and interface-trait associations downstream of the coding sequence [14]. A branch-model analysis across 30+ mammals would have greater power to detect lineage-specific acceleration, but should not be expected to show a gross gradient given the null pairwise result.

### Disease architecture: the gradient is topology-dependent, not trivially universal

The strongest alternative explanation for the disease burden gradient is that it reflects a trivially universal property of any pathway with shared upstream steps: disrupting a step used by every downstream branch will always produce a more severe phenotype than disrupting a step used by only one branch. On this view, the CDG concentration in upstream N-glyco genes says nothing distinctive about N-glycosylation's evolutionary architecture — it merely restates that shared steps are essential.

This alternative is directly tested by the comparator pathway analysis. In purine de novo synthesis — a linear pathway whose core enzymes (ADSL, ATIC) all share their product — both core and downstream branch enzymes (ADSS2, IMPDH1) cause severe Mendelian neurological disorders (Fisher p = 1.0). There is no gradient. In GPI-anchor biosynthesis, 19 of 20 pathway genes encode shared steps with no downstream diversification layer, and 95% have Mendelian disease associations — the whole pathway behaves like an N-glyco upstream core. In these linear pathways, disease is distributed uniformly, not concentrated, demonstrating that shared-step essentiality alone does not produce a gradient.

The gradient is also seen in the topology-matched comparator. Heparan sulfate biosynthesis has the same architectural feature as N-glycosylation: a conserved shared linker core (xylosyltransferases, galactosyltransferases, glucuronyltransferase, EXT1/2 chain polymerases) feeds a diversified downstream modification layer (NDST, HS2ST, HS3ST, HS6ST, SULF families with tissue-specific and acceptor-specific expression). In the current curation, every one of the 8 core HS genes causes a severe Mendelian skeletal or connective tissue disorder, while none of the 16 downstream modification genes has the same curated Mendelian disease status (Fisher p < 0.0001). This pattern is not sufficient to prove a universal principle, but it supports the specific prediction that shared-core and diversified-output topology can structure disease burden.

The disease evidence is conservative in the right direction: GeneReviews CDG curation is not exhaustive, and ClinVar evidence reflects ascertainment history. The absence of CDG evidence for most downstream genes does not mean they are clinically benign — B4GALT1 illustrates a downstream gene with both CDG annotation and extensive glycome GWAS evidence. The correct claim is not that downstream genes are disease-free but that severe Mendelian disruption concentrates in shared early machinery in a pattern that depends on pathway topology rather than essentiality alone. Follow-up work testing O-glycosylation or sialylation pathway architectures would determine whether the topology dependency generalizes beyond N-glycosylation and heparan sulfate.

### Downstream glycome and immune trait enrichment: architecture signal, not gene-density noise

The simplest alternative explanation for the downstream GWAS signal is that larger genes occupy more chromosomal territory, capture more LD, and therefore map to more GWAS associations by proximity — not because the genes themselves influence the traits. Under this explanation, the downstream glycome and immune enrichment is a gene-size artifact: longer genes accumulate more hits, and more hits increase the chance of picking up any given trait category by chance.

The genome-wide null model tests this directly. Using all 982,984 genome-wide significant associations from GWAS Catalog v1.0.2, we classified every trait as glycan_direct, immune_nonglycan, or other, and compared the downstream N-glyco layer against a background of 31,847 non-pathway genes. The critical observation is the asymmetry within the pathway: upstream N-glyco genes are depleted for glycan/immune hits relative to genome-wide background (OR=0.58), while downstream genes are enriched (OR=2.47, p=0.003). Both layers are in the same pathway and in the same LD environment; if the enrichment were purely a gene-size effect, both layers should show proportionally similar enrichment relative to background. They do not. When downstream genes are matched to background genes by total GWAS hit count — a direct proxy for gene length and LD capture — the enrichment weakens to OR=1.63 (p=0.107), confirming partial gene-length confounding. The asymmetry between upstream depletion and downstream enrichment remains, and a Mann-Whitney test for the full glycan/immune hit count distribution remains significant (p=3.6×10⁻⁴), indicating that the downstream layer carries disproportionately many glycan and immune hits beyond what gene size explains.

The strongest individual downstream examples are ST3GAL4 (118 immune-nonglycan associations, primarily leukocyte sialylation), FUT8 (171 direct IgG glycosylation associations), MGAT3 (81 N-glycan structure associations), and ST6GAL1 (67 direct glycosylation associations), each supported by direct glycome or immune trait text and reported-gene evidence. These genes encode enzymes that shape mature N-glycan structures at protein surfaces exposed to immune effectors, viral lectins, and inflammatory mediators, which is the biological context in which interface-layer variation is most plausibly relevant. The GWAS evidence supports prioritization and trait-layer interpretation, not causal adaptation claims.

The honest conclusion is mixed: the downstream trait enrichment is a layer-specific signal in current annotations, but the matched background analysis weakens to non-significance, indicating that gene-length or annotation-density confounding is substantial. The architecture claim is best stated as: the downstream layer's trait profile is qualitatively different from the upstream layer's, and that difference is not reduced to raw gene size alone.

A sharper test restricts the question to IgG glycan phenotypes specifically: among all 54 independent GWS IgG-glycosylation and N-glycan loci in the GWAS Catalog, how many are assigned to upstream versus downstream N-glyco pathway genes? Seven downstream N-glyco genes each contributed one independent assigned IgG glycan locus — FUT8 (mean |β| = 0.29), MGAT3 (0.30), ST6GAL1 (0.44), B4GALT1 (0.48), FUT3 (0.35), MGAT5 (0.25), and ST3GAL4 (0.52). Zero upstream N-glyco genes contributed an assigned independent IgG glycan locus (Fisher OR = ∞, p = 0.003). This is a strong spatial skew in the current locus-to-gene mapping, not proof that all causal variation is downstream. It is nevertheless consistent with both the CDG disease architecture and the idea that quantitative glycan-output variation is more visible in the downstream layer than in core precursor and transfer machinery. What would sharpen this result further is fine mapping and cis-eQTL colocalization for these seven loci to test causality, and heritability partitioning across the full IgG glycome, not just GWAS-significant loci, to quantify the total variance contributed by each pathway layer.


### Population-genetic analysis finds no coding-sweep gradient

FST (1000 Genomes Phase 3, ten superpopulation contrasts) and iHS (PopHuman, 26 populations) showed no statistically significant gradient between upstream-core and downstream-diversification genes. In the most-powered AFR-EUR contrast, median per-gene FST was 0.081 upstream and 0.085 downstream — not meaningfully different. This null result is consistent with downstream evolvability operating through regulatory and glycan-output channels rather than through differentiating coding sweeps, since per-gene mean FST is not optimised to detect soft sweeps or regulatory variation. Candidate outliers — FUT8 (iHS max 3.39 in AFR, 3.38 in EAS), MOGS (FST 0.285 in AFR-EUR) — are hypothesis-generating only and were not corrected for gene length, recombination rate, or demographic structure.


### What the evolvability claim does and does not require

The downstream layer could be merely non-catastrophic rather than genuinely evolvable: enzymes that are functionally marginal, redundant, or tissue-restricted might tolerate disruption without generating any heritable phenotypic variation. The IgG glycan locus result addresses this possibility at the trait-association layer. All seven N-glyco pathway genes assigned independent IgG glycan GWAS loci (FUT8, MGAT3, ST6GAL1, B4GALT1, FUT3, MGAT5, ST3GAL4) are downstream; zero upstream genes appear (OR = ∞, p = 0.003). This supports the claim that measurable glycan-output variation is concentrated downstream in current GWAS annotations. It does not by itself prove selection, adaptation, or complete absence of upstream regulatory effects.

The dN/dS null result (medians 0.075 vs. 0.073, p = 0.49) and the flat cross-species % identity argue against faster coding evolution as the main downstream signal. Evolvability in the downstream layer is therefore more plausibly expressed through glycan-output variation, regulatory control, and enzyme-family diversification than through relaxed purifying selection on protein-coding sequence [1,2,4]. The robustness/evolvability framework predicts this organisation: catastrophic failure points localised in a shared core, phenotypic variation organised through the interface output layer. What remains unresolved is whether the downstream GWAS loci reflect coding or regulatory variants (fine mapping with cis-eQTL colocalization is the natural next step), and whether this topology generalises to other glycosylation systems — a testable prediction of the model.

### Limitations

**GWAS gene assignments are not causal.** The downstream-vs-upstream enrichment (OR = 4.24) and IgG glycan spatial separation (OR = ∞, p = 0.003) reflect GWAS Catalog gene assignments based on reported or mapped genes and LD-window proximity. The matched background analysis weakens to OR = 1.63 (p = 0.107), confirming partial gene-density or annotation confounding. Per-locus causal assignments require fine mapping and cis-eQTL colocalization. The directional pattern is strong enough to prioritize the downstream layer, but both individual locus claims and aggregate causal interpretation remain provisional.

**Comparator pathways are not formally matched.** The comparators (purine, GPI, HS, ceramide/GSL) differ in gene count, expression breadth, and ascertainment history. The analysis establishes that the gradient is topology-dependent but does not formally control for non-topology covariates. The result is convergent evidence, not a matched experiment.

**LOEUF and gnomAD may understate recessive lethality.** The Wald survivor-bias interpretation is supported by matched CDS-length comparisons and apparent grading by disease severity, but it is not independently confirmed by family-level or functional data. The claim that some recessive lethal CDG genes look more tolerant by LOEUF because the most severe genotypes are absent from gnomAD is plausible and biologically coherent, but not the only possible explanation of the pattern.

### Conclusions

N-glycosylation's evolutionary architecture is spatially partitioned in a way visible through disease burden and glycan-output trait profiles but not through a simple coding-constraint or population-differentiation gradient. The current evidence supports a layered model in which shared precursor and transfer machinery carries high severe-disease burden, while downstream branching and terminal-modification genes carry more visible glycan-output and interface-trait associations. CDG and ClinVar disease burden is concentrated in early machinery (73% CDG fraction in LLO assembly) and low in Golgi branching in the current curation. Comparator analyses suggest that this gradient is topology-dependent, with a similar disease-burden separation in heparan sulfate biosynthesis and weaker separation in linear comparators. The genome-wide GWAS null model shows downstream enrichment for glycan and immune traits relative to upstream genes (OR = 4.24), while hit-count matching shows that gene-density confounding remains important. IgG glycan locus partitioning provides a direct trait-layer readout: all seven N-glyco pathway genes assigned independent IgG glycan loci are downstream (OR = ∞, p = 0.003), but causality remains to be tested by fine mapping and colocalization. The apparent LOEUF inversion is plausibly influenced by gene-length confounding and survivor sampling in recessive disease genes. Pairwise dN/dS, cross-species identity, and population-genetic FST/iHS are largely null across layers, supporting the narrower conclusion that downstream evolvability is more likely expressed through glycan-output and regulatory variation than through broad coding divergence.

The downstream layer is not simply weakly constrained; in the current GWAS Catalog-derived IgG glycan mapping, it is where measurable glycan-output trait associations concentrate. Whether this architecture generalises to other glycosylation pathways and biological systems is a testable prediction of the model.
