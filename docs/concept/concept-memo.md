# Concept Memo

## Working Thesis

N-glycosylation may provide a concrete case study for how biological pathways localize robustness and evolvability into different regions. Upstream core machinery supports conserved function, protein-folding quality control, and intolerance of damaging perturbation. Downstream branching and terminal modification may support tolerated variation, tissue-specific deployment, and environmental interaction at the cell surface.

## Competing Hypotheses

- The apparent upstream/downstream contrast reflects pathway architecture: catastrophic failure points are buffered from variation, while branching and interface layers permit evolutionary exploration.
- The contrast is mainly an annotation artifact caused by gene length, SNP density, constraint, expression breadth, recombination, background selection, or disease-study ascertainment.
- N-glycosylation is not special; similar gradients occur across many metabolic and interface pathways.
- The original population-genetic signals were largely demography, LD, array ascertainment, or window-assignment artifacts.

## Initial Predictions

- Upstream genes should show stronger evolutionary constraint and higher severe Mendelian disease burden.
- Downstream genes should show greater tolerated standing variation and richer links to immune, inflammatory, infection, cancer, microbiome, and tissue-identity phenotypes.
- Network positions with high catastrophic potential should be depleted for tolerated damaging variation.
- Redundant, branching, tissue-specific, and terminal modification regions should be enriched for innovation-zone signals.

## Target Figure Set

- Pathway architecture schematic separating conserved core, checkpoint/processing layer, diversification layer, and environmental interface layer.
- Constraint and disease-burden gradients across pathway regions.
- Trait-association profile comparing upstream and downstream genes.
- Network topology figure showing depth, branching, redundancy, and quality-control checkpoints.
- Evidence matrix distinguishing robust findings from hypothesis-generating candidate signals.
